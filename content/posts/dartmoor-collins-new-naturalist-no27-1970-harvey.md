## The Enduring Allure of the Collins New Naturalist Series

If you've spent any time collecting natural history books, you’ll know the *Collins New Naturalist* series. For many of us, it’s not just *a* series, it’s *the* series. Launched right after World War II in 1945, these books weren't just pretty picture books; they were a foundational effort to survey, document, and truly understand the natural landscapes of the UK. Each volume became a record, a snapshot of its subject matter, crafted with both scientific precision and a deep love for the natural world.

What gets me every time is the sheer ambition of the project. Britain was recovering, looking inward, and finding pride in its own unique ecology. These books gave people a way to connect with that. They weren't dumbed down; they were written by leading experts in their fields, people who knew their subjects inside out. But they were also incredibly accessible, inviting laypeople to explore everything from the habits of British mammals to the specific flora of a particular region.

For collectors, there are so many layers of appeal. First, the intellectual content is top-notch. These books are still referenced today, holding up as solid works of ecological scholarship. Second, the production quality. Collins didn't skimp. The bindings, the paper, the printing – it’s all built to last, which is why we’re still finding copies in fantastic condition decades later. Then there are those dust jackets. Often designed by artists like Clifford and Rosemary Ellis, they're instantly recognizable and, for many, a huge part of the series’ visual identity. They elevate the books from mere academic texts to works of art in their own right, making them something you genuinely want to display on your shelves. In my experience picking these up over the years, a good dust jacket can easily double or triple the value of a clean copy, just because it completes the aesthetic.

## Dartmoor: A Deep Dive into the Moor

Volume 27, "Dartmoor," takes us right into the wild heart of Devon, exploring one of Britain’s most iconic and mysterious landscapes. Written by L.A. Harvey and D. St Leger-Gordon, this book is more than just a guidebook; it's a comprehensive ecological study. They don't just tell you *what* lives on the moor; they explain *why* it does, delving into the geology, the climate, the historical human impact, and the intricate web of life that thrives there.

Harvey, a zoologist, and St Leger-Gordon, a botanist and local expert, were the perfect team to tackle such a complex subject. They bring a scientist’s eye to the unique challenges and adaptations of Dartmoor's flora and fauna – from the tough moorland plants that withstand the harsh winds and acidic soils, to the specialized insects and birds that call this place home. When you read their descriptions, you don’t just learn facts; you feel the raw, untamed spirit of the moor. You can practically hear the wind whipping across the tors and smell the damp earth.

What I personally appreciate about books like this within the series is their focus on regional studies. While other volumes might cover broader subjects like "British Birds" or "Fungi," "Dartmoor" grounds you in a very specific place. It creates a detailed portrait, making you feel like you've spent weeks tramping across the heather yourself. It's this granular detail, combined with the broader ecological context, that makes these specific regional volumes so enduringly popular with collectors and enthusiasts alike. They offer a window into a specific place at a specific time, capturing the environmental studies efforts of the mid-20th century.

## The Specifics of *This* Edition: More Than Just a Date

Now, let's get down to the nitty-gritty of *this particular copy*. When you’re collecting *Collins New Naturalist* books, the edition and print run can tell you a lot – and they can drastically affect value. My copy of "Dartmoor" is a 1970 reprint of the Revised Second Edition, which itself was originally published in 1962. The true first edition, for those chasing the earliest copies, dates all the way back to 1949.

So, why bother with a 1970 reprint when the first is out there? Well, for starters, the 1949 first edition is notoriously hard to find in anything approaching decent condition, and when you do, it commands a hefty premium. More importantly, this 1970 reprint has its own unique points of interest that make it a fascinating piece for the discerning collector.

The biggest giveaway for this specific print run is right there on the dust jacket: the price. It’s listed as £2.25. Now, for those unfamiliar with British currency history, this is a decimalized price. The UK officially switched to decimal currency in February 1971. This means that while the book itself says "1970 reprint," it was either printed in very late 1970 for sale immediately following decimalization, or more likely, issued or sold sometime *after* February 1971. This little detail isn't just a quirky historical note; it’s a concrete bibliographic marker, a timestamp that tells you exactly when this particular batch hit the shelves. It distinguishes it immediately from earlier 1962 or 1949 printings, which would have carried prices in shillings and pence (like "45s" or similar).

Another important clue for us collectors is the presence of an SBN (Standard Book Number), which later evolved into the ISBN. This was a relatively new system being adopted around the late 1960s and early 1970s. Its inclusion further confirms this copy's place in time, cementing it as a product of that specific publishing era. These little details might seem small to some, but for those of us who appreciate the history of books and printing, they're like decoding a secret message from the past. They provide a precise anchor point in the often-murky waters of reprints and editions.

So, even though the book itself states a "1970 reprint" year, that decimal price acts as a precise dating feature, confirming its post-decimalization issue. It's a key marker that distinguishes it from earlier printings.
