## Unearthing Galloway: Historical Context of a 1930s Masterpiece

Published in 1932, *The Spell of its Hills and Glens* emerged during a fascinating, albeit challenging, period in history. The global economic downturn of the Great Depression compelled many to find solace and adventure closer to home, fostering a renewed appreciation for regional exploration and the natural landscapes of one's own country. Andrew McCormick's work was perfectly poised to capture this zeitgeist, offering a meticulously detailed and passionately written guide to the enchanting Galloway region of Scotland.

McCormick's motivations stretched beyond mere tourism; he sought to immortalize the unique character, history, and natural beauty of Galloway. His writing style is imbued with a deep personal connection to the land, painting vivid pictures of its rolling hills, dramatic glens, serene lochs, and vibrant local culture. This was not a superficial guide but a loving testament to a region often overlooked in favor of its more famous northern counterparts. For those interested in Scottish regional history, McCormick's dedication to preserving the essence of Galloway through his prose makes this book an invaluable primary source.

What makes this particular historical context even more intriguing is the subtle nod to technological advancement embedded within a book largely dedicated to traditional walking and exploration. The inclusion of an "Aeroplane route" on the accompanying map reflects a fascinating blend of the old and the new. While hiking and walking guides had long been staples for adventurers, the burgeoning field of aviation was transforming perceptions of travel and geography. To see an early 20th-century publication bridge these two seemingly disparate modes of exploration—footpaths through ancient hills alongside routes for aerial navigation—offers a unique snapshot of the era's evolving relationship with the landscape. It speaks to a moment when the thrill of discovering hidden corners of Scotland was amplified by the dawning age of flight, even if only in conceptual terms for most readers. This nuanced reflection of its time elevates *The Spell of its Hills and Glens* from a simple regional guide to a significant historical artifact.

## Navigating the Terrain: Comparable Editions and Versions

When evaluating the rarity and value of any antiquarian book, understanding its lineage of editions and printings is paramount. For Andrew McCormick's *The Spell of its Hills and Glens*, the distinction between different versions is particularly acute, especially concerning its most coveted feature: the original E.F. Inkster map.

It is important to note that later printings of *The Spell of its Hills and Glens* do exist. As McCormick's work found an appreciative audience, the book was reprinted to meet demand. However, these subsequent editions typically fall short of the collector appeal of an early, map-complete copy like the December 1932 printing. The primary reason for this diminished desirability stems from two critical factors: the presence and condition of the map, and the overall state of the book itself.

Many later printings either lack the original large-scale folding map entirely, substituting it with a smaller, less detailed, or more generic map, or feature a map that is significantly degraded in condition. Over time, original maps—being folded, unfolded, and handled—are often the first components of a book to suffer damage, become detached, or even go missing. Copies with maps that are foxed, torn, crudely repaired, or incomplete substantially lose their appeal to serious collectors. The uniqueness of Inkster's cartography, with its "Aeroplane route" and charming vignettes, makes the original map irreplaceable. A later printing with a different map or no map at all simply does not offer the same historical insight or artistic value.

Furthermore, later printings are, by their very nature, less rare than the initial run. While they serve as perfectly adequate reading copies for casual readers or general history enthusiasts, they do not possess the bibliographic significance of an early printing. The "First Edition Typeface" designation of the December 1932 copy marks it as being from the original production run, making it a prime example of collectible regional history. Later printings, while still containing McCormick's prose, generally lack this specific early printing identifier, thus rendering them less desirable from a collector's standpoint.

The overall condition of later printings is also frequently of a lesser standard. Books that have been through multiple print runs and longer periods of circulation are more likely to exhibit signs of wear and tear—faded covers, loosened bindings, marginalia, or general signs of age that detract from their aesthetic and monetary value.

In summary, while *The Spell of its Hills and Glens* continued to be printed, the unparalleled insight, original artistry, and historical fidelity offered by an early printing, particularly one that retains the pristine E.F. Inkster map, significantly outstrip the collector appeal of any comparable later edition or version. Collectors are keen to acquire the earliest, most complete, and best-preserved examples, understanding that these represent the truest form of the work as it was originally conceived and released.
