## Historical Background: A Confluence of Eras and Eroticism

To truly appreciate the 1932 Three Sirens Press edition of *Aphrodite & Lysistrata*, we must first delve into the rich historical and literary currents that converged to create it. This volume brings together two works, each scandalous and celebrated in its own right, presented through the lens of early 20th-century artistic sensibilities.

Pierre Louÿs (1870-1925) was a celebrated French poet and novelist, often associated with the Symbolist and Decadent movements of the late 19th century. His writing was characterized by a lush, evocative style, deeply sensual and often pushing the boundaries of what was considered acceptable in literature. Louÿs had a profound fascination with classical antiquity, viewing it as a period of uninhibited passion and beauty, far removed from the perceived prudishness of his own time. His most famous novel, *Aphrodite: Ancient Manners* (published in 1896), is a vivid exploration of love, desire, and the intricate social dynamics set against the backdrop of ancient Alexandria. The narrative follows the beautiful courtesan Chrysis as she navigates a world where beauty is currency and pleasure is paramount. Upon its release, *Aphrodite* became an instant sensation, both for its literary merit and its frank, unapologetic depiction of eroticism. So explicit were its descriptions of ancient sexuality that it faced obscenity charges in some jurisdictions, cementing Louÿs's reputation as a daring and controversial author. Despite – or perhaps because of – this notoriety, *Aphrodite* has endured as a landmark work of fin-de-siècle literature, celebrated for its poetic language and its fearless embrace of human sensuality.

The other half of this compelling duo is *Lysistrata*, an ancient Greek comedy by the master playwright Aristophanes (c. 411 BC). Unlike Louÿs’s work, which channels historical fantasy, *Lysistrata* is a pointed political satire with a timeless premise. Set during the long-running Peloponnesian War, the play depicts the radical actions of Lysistrata, an Athenian woman who persuades the women of Greece to withhold sex from their husbands until peace is negotiated. This "sex strike" forms the central comedic and dramatic tension of the play, exploring themes of gender roles, war, and political power through biting wit, absurd situations, and strong sexual innuendo. While ancient, Aristophanes’s work resonates through the ages for its clever commentary on human nature and its enduring message against the futility of war. Its comedic portrayal of sexual politics, much like *Aphrodite*'s overt eroticism, ensures its continued relevance and occasional controversy.

The publishing house responsible for this unique compilation, Three Sirens Press, was an American entity active from the early to mid-20th century. They carved out a niche for themselves by producing attractively bound and illustrated editions of literary classics, often with a focus on works possessing a romantic, exotic, or slightly "risqué" appeal. Their publications were designed not just to be read but to be admired as objects of beauty, making them particularly appealing to collectors of fine editions.

This specific edition, published in 1932, sits firmly within the interwar period, a time of significant cultural shifts and artistic innovation. The Roaring Twenties had just given way to the Great Depression, but the appetite for luxury, escapism, and sophisticated artistry remained. This was the era of Art Deco, a design movement that swept across architecture, fashion, and book illustration. Art Deco, with its distinctive geometric patterns, streamlined forms, rich colors, and often exotic motifs, perfectly captured the spirit of modernity and elegance.

It is against this backdrop that Frank J. Buttera's Art Deco illustrations come into play. Buttera was an artist whose work perfectly encapsulated the popular aesthetic trends of the 1920s and 1930s. His illustrations for this edition are not mere adornments; they are an integral part of the book's identity, merging the modern artistic sensibilities of his time with the timeless, often provocative, narratives of Louÿs and Aristophanes. Buttera's style provides a visual bridge between ancient worlds and the contemporary collector's eye, making this 1932 edition a fascinating artifact of its period. The decision to combine these two "classic yet risqué" texts, accompanied by such striking Art Deco visuals, speaks volumes about the tastes and desires of the reading public and the discerning eye of Three Sirens Press during this fascinating historical juncture.
